THE PREMISE
The Premise

A house in Connemara that knows how to wait.

Connemara was emptied once already. In the years after the Hunger, whole townlands fell silent — roofless gables, hearths gone cold, footpaths grown over with bracken and bog cotton. The wind kept moving through them. The walls stayed where they were. The land learned the particular grief of holding a place that nobody had the strength to inhabit.

This is the part of Ireland that knows what to do with a worn body. Not because it offers comfort — Connemara is not a comfortable place — but because it has held empty cottages without judgement for a hundred and seventy years, and a body that arrives there exhausted is met by a landscape that has been waiting, attentively, for someone to come back.

Suaimhneas is a proposal for three lime-washed stone cottages, rebuilt around a sheltered courtyard on a south-facing hillside between mountain and sea, and held open in perpetuity for the people who would never otherwise reach such a place: nurses, carers, cancer survivors, parents of dying children, frontline workers whose nervous systems have given way.

It is not a clinic. It is a house in Connemara that knows how to wait.

COMMITMENTS
Three Commitments

A house, in this place, must keep its word.

i.

Free, in perpetuity

Eligibility is by referral — from oncology services, hospice teams, palliative-care networks, frontline-worker support charities. No guest is asked what they earn or what they can pay.

ii.

Long enough to mean something

Stays are six to twelve weeks. A weekend cannot undo what a year has done to the body. The body needs slowness more than it needs intensity.

iii.

Built into the parish

Suaimhneas is a member of its townland — buying from local farms, employing local craftspeople. The house exists inside Connemara, not on top of it.

SIX PILLARS
What Makes the Place

Six things, held in one hand.

— 01

The Land

A south-facing hillside between Roundstone and Clifden, halfway between the Twelve Bens and the Atlantic at Mannin Bay. The land does not need to be improved. It needs to be quietly kept.

— 02

The Architecture

Three single-storey cottages in local fieldstone, lime-washed white in the old way, set in a clachan around a sheltered courtyard. Walls eighteen inches thick. Slate roofs. Twin gable-end chimneys. In conversation with Ryan W. Kennihan, Tom de Paor, Tierney Haines.

— 03

The Palette

Limewash white. Granite grey. Lichen grey-green. Rust-brown of an old gate hinge. Peat-dark. Slate-blue of Mannin Bay. Floors in flagstone and oak. Donegal tweed, Galway wool, Foxford linen.

— 04

The People

A small resident team — clinical lead, two body-workers, cook, gardener, housekeeper, parish liaison. The Irish word suíocháin — the act of sitting with — is, in this part of the country, an old skill, intact.

— 05

The Parish

Vegetables from Carna and Ballyconneely. Lamb from the next townland. Bread baked on site. The thatcher, stonemason, carpenter, ironworker — all local.

— 06

The Land's Recovery

Carbon-negative by design. Sphagnum recovery on the upland bog. Fifteen thousand newly planted native broadleaves. A place of recovery should itself be in recovery.

THE CLACHAN
The Place

Three cottages around a sheltered courtyard.

The clachan sits on a south-facing hillside between Roundstone and Clifden. Three cottages turn their backs to the prevailing wind and their faces to one another.

Cottage A — The Hearth

Common rooms, library, dining hall, kitchen, four practitioner studios. The deep central hearth that is never allowed to go out.

Cottages B and C — Sleeping

Three guest rooms in each, six in total. Every room has a deep window seat looking south, a half-door to the courtyard.

The Bath House

A small fourth building, set apart in the lee of the hill. Stone, water, fire, peat-steam. Outdoor cold plunge.

A DAY HERE
A Day at Suaimhneas

No schedule. A rhythm.

The house does not programme its guests. It offers a frame — the slow, repeating shape of a Connemara day.

07:00

The hearth quickened

The turf fire, banked the night before, is opened up. Tea is set in the kitchen.

08:30

Breakfast in silence

Brown soda bread, eggs from the henhouse, smoked mackerel from Roundstone Quay.

10:30

Hands and bodies

One-to-one sessions in the practice cottage. Craniosacral. Acupuncture. Conversation.

13:30

The long lunch

Vegetables from the walled garden, lamb from the next townland. The afternoon is not earned.

15:00

The walk, or not

The bog road, the cliff path at Dog's Bay, the boreen down to the strand. The wind decides.

19:30

Dinner together

The whole house at one table by the turf fire. Candlelight. A fiddle, sometimes.

22:00

The house quiets

The fire is banked for the night. The wind takes over. The body accepts the invitation to sleep.

WHO COMES
Who Comes

Not the worried well. The genuinely emptied.

Suaimhneas is the next room after the hospital, for people who would never otherwise reach Connemara.

People recovering from cancer

Treatment finished, technically well, facing the long aftermath that medical systems are not built to hold.

Long-term carers

Parents, partners, adult children who have spent years caring — and have arrived at the end of it, depleted in ways no one has named for them.

Frontline professionals

Nurses, doctors, hospice workers, social workers, teachers — those whose vocation has cost them their nervous system.

The bereaved

People in the long, quiet phase of grief — months and years after the funeral, when the world has moved on and they have not.

Survivors of catastrophic events

People emerging from the acute phase of accident, displacement, or violence, who need a place to be slowly held.

The chronically burnt out

Those whose collapse has been quiet, gradual, undramatic — and therefore largely invisible.

AN INVITATION
An Invitation

Perhaps a hundred people
could make this real.

Suaimhneas is not seeking a return on investment. It is seeking the rare kind of capital that wishes to leave behind something that does not require it to keep performing.

A founding endowment of approximately twenty-five million euros funds the house in perpetuity.

The intention is not a chain. It is one house, in one place, held to one standard.

— i

Founding Patron

Sole or co-lead funder of the founding endowment. Naming of the principal hearth in Cottage A, and a permanent seat on the trust.

— ii

Cottage Endower

Endowment of one of the three cottages — five to seven million euros — covering build and a hundred-year operational reserve.

— iii

Stone Circle

Twenty founding contributors, each at one million euros, forming the standing trust.

We are not building a clinic.
We are building a stone house in Connemara
that knows how to wait.
